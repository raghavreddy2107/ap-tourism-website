Vizianagaram

Vizianagaram District, located in the northeastern part of Andhra Pradesh, is a region with a rich history and cultural heritage, formed on June 1, 1979, by carving out areas from the neighboring Srikakulam and Visakhapatnam districts. Historically, it was part of the ancient Kalinga region, and its past is marked by significant events such as the fierce "Bobbili Yuddham" (Bobbili War) of 1757, a legendary battle between the Rajas of Vizianagaram and Bobbili, which is still celebrated for its tales of bravery and sacrifice. The district's headquarters, Vizianagaram city, is known as the "City of Victory" and was founded in 1712 by the Vizianagaram Rajas, who were patrons of education and the arts. Today, Vizianagaram District is a blend of historical landmarks, religious sites, and natural beauty. Prominent attractions include the Vizianagaram Fort, built in 1713, and the Bobbili Fort, both standing as testaments to the region's royal past. Religious tourism thrives with temples like Pydithalli Ammavari Temple, Ramatheertham, famous for its ancient Rama temple and Buddhist relics, and the unique Ramanarayanam Temple, designed in the shape of a bow and arrow. Nature enthusiasts can explore the Thatipudi Reservoir, a scenic spot on the Gosthani River, and various waterfalls like Alakananda Waterfalls. The district is also known for its Bobbili Veena, a traditional Saraswati Veena renowned for its craftsmanship. With its historical significance, cultural vibrancy, and picturesque locations, Vizianagaram District offers a captivating experience for visitors seeking to delve into Andhra Pradesh's rich legacy.

Temples

Pydithalli Ammavari Temple


The Pydithalli Ammavari Temple, located in Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, is a significant Hindu shrine dedicated to Goddess Pydithalli Ammavaru, revered as the local deity. Its origins are linked to the 1757 Bobbili War, with legend stating that Princess Pydimamba, sister of the Vizianagaram king, died on the same day as her brother in the conflict. Her spirit is believed to have later guided a soldier to find her idol in a lake, leading to the temple's 18th-century construction. The temple's primary highlight is the annual Sirimanu Utsavam (Sirimanotsavam), a grand festival held on the first Tuesday after Vijayadashami (Dussehra), where a tall, decorated wooden pole (Sirimanu) is paraded, symbolizing the Goddess's presence and drawing thousands of devotees and tourists.

Ramanarayanam


Ramanarayanam in Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, is a unique 15-acre spiritual theme park and temple complex built by the NCS Charitable Trust. Its most striking feature is its architecture, shaped like a colossal bow and arrow, symbolizing Lord Rama and the Ramayana. Inside this two-storied structure, 72 sculpted mouldings depict key episodes from the epic. Beyond the main temple, it includes a traditional Vishnu temple, sacred plant sections, a library, and a major attraction: a Hanuman Laser Show on a 100-foot Hanuman statue, narrating parts of the Hanuman Chalisa and Ramayana. Open generally from 10 AM to 9 PM, Ramanarayanam offers a unique blend of spirituality, culture, and education, making it a significant draw for visitors to the region.

Mannar Rajagopala Swamy Temple


The Sri Mannar Rajagopala Swamy Temple in Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, is an 800-year-old Vaishnavite shrine, locally known as Santhaana Gopala Swamy Temple. Believed to be guided by Sri Ramanujacharya, it uniquely follows the Pancha Raatra Agama for its rituals. The temple houses Lord Rajagopala (Krishna) with Rukmini and Satyabhama, alongside ancient stone idols from an earlier site. Its architecture includes mandapams with detailed carvings and Ramayana paintings. The annual five-day Kalyanotsavam is its main festival, drawing many devotees seeking blessings.

Gnana Saraswathi Temple


The Gnana Saraswathi Devi Temple in Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, built around 2008, is a revered shrine dedicated to Goddess Saraswati, patron of knowledge and arts. Designed in traditional South Indian architectural style, it's particularly popular for the Aksharabhyasam ceremony, where children are initiated into learning. Major festivals like Vasant Panchami and Navaratri are celebrated with great enthusiasm. Located in SVN Nagar, it's easily accessible and open generally from 6:00 AM to 8:30 PM, serving as a significant cultural and educational hub in the region.

Kanyaka Parameshwari Temple


The Kanyaka Parameswari Temple in Vizianagaram, built in 1890, is a revered shrine dedicated to Goddess Kanyaka Parameswari, the clan deity of the Komati (Arya Vysya) community. Its construction honors a legendary community girl who sacrificed her life for honor. The temple exhibits traditional South Indian architecture. It's a focal point for devotees seeking blessings and celebrates major festivals like Vasavi Jayanti, which commemorates the goddess's birth, and Navaratri. General timings are 6:00 AM-12:00 PM and 5:00 PM-8:00 PM.

Jagannatha Temple


The Jagannatha Temple in Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, is an ancient shrine dedicated to Lord Jagannath, Balabhadra, and Subhadra, believed to be over 800 years old. It holds a unique legend: its idols were purportedly retrieved from the sea after the traditional immersion of Puri Jagannath's replicas. Built entirely of stone, the temple features intricately carved pillars and follows the Pancha Raatra Agama. The wooden deities, miraculously preserved for centuries, undergo annual re-consecration before the grand Rath Yatra, the temple's main festival. Other important festivals include Vaikuntha Ekadasi, Ugadi, and Janmashtami. Recently restored by local efforts, the temple is open from 5:00 AM to 8:30 PM.

Ramatheertham


Ramatheertham, near Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, is a historically rich site featuring an ancient Sri Rama Temple on Bavikonda hill, believed to be over 1,000 years old, where Lord Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana are worshipped. This spiritual hub is also remarkably significant for its extensive archaeological remains of ancient Buddhist and Jain monasteries, stupas, and rock-cut caves found on the surrounding Bodhikonda and Durga Konda hills, dating back to the 3rd Century BCE. The temple, built on a huge rock, observes major festivals like Sri Ramanavami. Despite a recent unfortunate act of vandalism on the Rama idol, the site remains a vital pilgrimage and historical destination, generally open from 5:00 AM to 8:00 PM.

Bobbili Venugopala Swamy Temple


The Bobbili Venugopala Swamy Temple in Bobbili, Vizianagaram, is an ancient shrine dedicated to Lord Krishna as Venugopala, dating back centuries and patronized by the Bobbili Rajas. The temple features a blend of South Indian architectural styles, with intricate carvings and sculptures depicting Krishna playing the flute. The annual Kalyanotsavam is a major festival. The temple is also linked to the Bobbili Veena. It's generally open from 6:00 AM to 12:00 PM and 4:00 PM to 8:00 PM.

Saripalli Dibbi Lingeswara Swamy Temple


The Saripalli Dibbi Lingeswara Temple in Saripalli village, Vizianagaram District, Andhra Pradesh, is an ancient Shiva temple, possibly dating back to the Eastern Ganga dynasty, featuring a revered Lingam. Its name "Dibbi" suggests its origin on an elevated mound. The temple, built with traditional stone architecture, serves as a vital local religious center. Its main festival is Maha Shivaratri, attracting numerous devotees. The temple is generally open for morning and evening pujas and is accessible by road in Saripalli village.

Forts and Monuments

Vizianagaram Fort


Vizianagaram Fort, built in 1713-1718 by Maharaja Vijaya Rama Raju I, served as the stronghold of the Vizianagaram dynasty. Its architecture blends indigenous and European styles, featuring strong walls and gateways. The Nagara Khana is a notable entrance. Inside, the Mutyalaamba Temple remains significant. The fort, a witness to regional conflicts, reflects the cultural patronage of the Vizianagaram Rajas. It is a major landmark in Vizianagaram, open to visitors during daylight hours.

Bobbili War Memorial


The Bobbili War Memorial in Bobbili, Vizianagaram, commemorates the tragic yet heroic Battle of Bobbili (January 24, 1757), fought between Bobbili and Vizianagaram forces allied with the French. It honors the Bobbili warriors and people who chose death over surrender, including the mass self-sacrifice of women and the vengeful assassination of the Vizianagaram Raja by Tandra Paparayudu, the "Tiger of Bobbili." The memorial stands as a poignant symbol of valor, self-respect, and sacrifice, with annual tributes paid to the martyrs.

Alakananda Palace


Alakananda Palace, built in 1857 within the historic Vizianagaram Fort complex, was originally constructed as a royal guesthouse for the Pusapati rulers to host important dignitaries. Known for its elegant architecture, manicured gardens, and rare inclusion of a private airstrip, the palace reflects the opulence of its era. Although its interiors are currently inaccessible to the public—serving as the headquarters of the 5th Battalion of the Andhra Pradesh Armed Reserve Police—visitors to the fort can still admire its majestic façade and surroundings. Situated alongside other heritage structures like Moti Mahal and Oudh Khana, the palace is a key landmark showcasing the royal legacy and architectural grandeur of Vizianagaram.

Korukonda Palace


Korukonda Palace, originally known as Alak Appala Kondayamba Vijayaram Palace, was built in 1911 by Maharaja Vijayaram Gajapathi Raju as his serene country residence. In 1961, the palace and its sprawling 1,000‑acre estate were generously donated by the royal family to establish Sainik School Korukonda—a premier defence preparatory institution under the Ministry of Defence. Today, this magnificent heritage building serves as the administrative heart and central hall of the school, where cadets are trained for entry into academies like the NDA and INA. With its impressive architecture set amid landscaped gardens and extensive sports grounds, Korukonda Palace seamlessly blends regal legacy with educational purpose, nurturing future leaders in a setting steeped in history.

Gurajada Apparao House


Gurajada Apparao’s house in Vizianagaram—situated close to the city fort—is a charming late-19th-century two-storey building now recognised as a state-protected heritage monument and functioning as a memorial library. This modest yet graceful structure, featuring arched verandahs and a central courtyard supported by ornate columns, preserves the lived space of the Telugu literary pioneer who authored Kanyasulkam here in 1892. Having undergone a delicate restoration in 2018 with government funding to maintain its original character , the house offers visitors a quiet, evocative glimpse into Apparao’s world—a serene sanctuary that celebrates his enduring influence on Telugu language and social reform.

Bobbili Veena


The Bobbili Veena is an exquisite, large plucked string instrument from the town of Bobbili in Vizianagaram district, revered in Carnatic classical music and earning a Geographical Indication (GI) tag in 2011–12 for its unique craftsmanship. Crafted entirely from a single piece of jackfruit wood—also called the “ekanda” method—it features a lion‑head carved end, bell‑metal frets, and a resonator made from a hollowed gourd, resulting in a light yet sonorous Saraswati veena. Its origins trace back to the 17th century under Pedda Rayudu’s reign, with a distinct local design asserted during a challenge by Vizianagaram craftsman Musanna Chinayya in the late 19th century, resulting in a longer, full‑toned veena (~54″ compared to the 42″ potti veena). The art of Bobbili Veena has been sustained by the Sarvasiddhi community of Gollapalli village for over three centuries—forming the Bobbili Veena sampradayam—with around 30–40 artisanal families still carrying on the tradition

Beaches, Waterfalls , Reservoirs

Chintapalli Beach


Chintapalli (or Chintapalli NGF) Beach is a serene, somewhat secluded coastal gem about 30 km from Vizianagaram, nestled along the Bay of Bengal. Featuring golden sands, soft waves ideal for swimming, and a picturesque long pier with nearby lighthouse views, it’s a favorite spot for relaxing strolls, picnics, and local fishing scenes. While there are no major food or accommodation services directly on-site, day trips are easy by car or APSRTC bus ,The beach is generally clean, moderately visited—especially during festivals like Kartika Purnima—and offers a peaceful escape from busier Andhra beaches. A recent “Yogandhra” mass‑yoga event also underlines its role as a local community hub

Thatipudi Reservoir


Thatipudi (Tatipudi) Reservoir, built between 1963 and 1968 across the Gosthani River in Gantyada Mandal, Vizianagaram district, is both a vital irrigation channel—covering around 15,378 acres—and a scenic freshwater source for Visakhapatnam city. Nestled amid rolling hills and lush greenery, it has become a popular spot for picnics, boating, birdwatching, and trekking . Recent developments include the launch of speed boats, water taxis, and adventure water - sports operating daily from 9AM to 6PM, aiming to elevate it as a key tourism destination in the region. With a capacity of approximately 3.3 TMC, the reservoir not only supports farming villages across Gantyada, Jami, and S. Kota but also embodies the region's blend of natural beauty and functional utility.

Alakananda Waterfalls


Alakananda Waterfalls, nestled in the lush hills near Vizianagaram, Andhra Pradesh, is a serene, multi-tiered cascade plunging over rocky terrain—an understated gem often enjoyed by local nature enthusiasts. While not among AP’s tallest or most famous falls, it charms visitors with its tranquil ambiance, trekking-friendly trails, and pristine surroundings. Ideal to visit during or just after the monsoon when water flow peaks, the falls make for a peaceful day trip—perfect for picnics, photography, and a refreshing escape in verdant woodlands . Facilities are minimal, so come prepared with sturdy footwear, water, and snacks, and take care to leave no trace.

Festivals

Sirimanutsavam


Sirimanotsavam is the electrifying annual folk festival of Vizianagaram, held on the first Tuesday after Dussehra to honor Goddess Pydithalli Ammavaru. The centerpiece is the towering Sirimanu—a 55–60‑foot wooden pole selected through a divine vision. Atop this log sits the head priest, believed to be possessed by the goddess, and is carried in procession three times between the temple and the fort, drawing crowds of 200,000 to 400,000 devotees.

Sambara Jathara


The Sambara Polamamba Jatara, held annually in Sambara village (Makkuva Mandal, Parvathipuram Manyam, Vizianagaram district), celebrates Polamamba (also known as Polamma), a revered village deity. With roots as a traditional gramadevata (village goddess) festival, it has grown to become a major state-recognized event, drawing lakhs of devotees from North Andhra and neighbouring Odisha. Held over three days in late January (typically around the 27th–29th), the festival features vibrant religious rituals at the Polamamba Temple, elaborate ceremonial processions, and a festive village fair. The local administration organizes the event with extensive infrastructure—bus transport, lighting, sanitation, medical camps, drinking water, designated parking, pathways, and strict bans on plastic—to ensure the comfort and safety of the crowd
